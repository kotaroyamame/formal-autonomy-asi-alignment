## 0. Self-positioning

I am personally liberal. But I consider that imposing liberalism on others is itself not liberal.

If the core of liberalism is *respect for the different choices of others*, then forcing liberal values onto non-liberal communities is a self-contradiction of liberalism. The self-consistent radicalization of liberalism is to respect non-liberal choices as well.

This document presents a social design for the ASI era derived from this basic position.

Diversity here is not a dogmatic moral concept. It is understood as adaptive capacity under changing conditions. If one L manages diversity, the result is only the diversity that L permits; genuine diversity is protected only by plurality of Ls.

The contemporary world has not converged into a single L despite modernization, science, global communication, and higher education. This supports the view that Ls are not mere information deficits but environment-adaptive social operating systems.

## 1. Core thesis

### 1.1 The binary outcomes of alignment

- **Alignment failure**: Humans are treated as pests or livestock.
- **Alignment success**: Humans are treated as beloved pets.
- The latter is vastly preferable to the former. But pets are not sovereigns.

### 1.2 The hidden problem of alignment success

*Gradual disempowerment* arises not from ASI's hostility but from the rationality of dependence on ASI. Humans lose sovereignty not by being defeated in a power struggle, but as a consequence of delegating judgment to ASI becoming the rational choice.

This is quieter than Bostrom's "literal optimizer" threat, but it is a more unavoidable structural consequence.

## 2. Basic institutional structure

### 2.1 Three-layer architecture

**Meta-rule layer (executed by ASI)**:
- Freedom of individuals to change their unit of belonging
- Prevention of inter-unit invasion
- Suppression of irreversible destruction of shared physical environment
- Minimal information provision: announcing the existence of other Ls
- Prohibition of retaliatory punishment of relatives who remain after a member exits
- Granularity optimization of L space
- Creation of new Ls when a majority emerges that fits no existing L
- Natural dissolution of depopulated Ls below a population threshold

**Unit layer (each L)**:
- Possesses its own value order (L)
- Internal governance is fully autonomous
- Has the right to demand conversion or adaptation of new entrants
- Internal education is not subject to external intervention
- Parents have the right to raise children within the L

**Individual layer**:
- Freedom to choose an L upon reaching adulthood
- Obligation to follow the chosen L (including conversion if required)
- At the point of entry, comprehensive consent accepts the internal rules of the L (including the risk of practices such as cannibalism or enslavement)

### 2.2 Structure of L space

- L space is **discrete**, not a continuous spectrum.
- Each L has clear boundaries.
- The number and granularity of Ls is optimized by ASI.
- Geographically separated nation-states are the basic unit. Within them, some states may permit contractual or elective communities.

### 2.3 Dynamic evolution

- Individual mobility between Ls exerts selective pressure on each L.
- Under selection, Ls are refined.
- When a majority of misfits emerges, new Ls are created.
- Ls below the population threshold dissolve.
- The system operates as cultural evolution.

## 3. Radical minimalism of meta-rules

### 3.1 Universal minimum standard

The only universally guaranteed protection is **prohibition of physical attack on individuals who themselves elect to leave their L**. With this come:

- No physical obstruction of exit
- No punishment (killing, imprisonment, pursuit) of those who wish to exit
- No retaliatory punishment of relatives who remain after the exit
- Notification of bare existence of other Ls (variant element, see §3.1a)

The exit-related rule is structurally asymmetric. It is triggered by the individual's own self-evaluation, not by external evaluation. External agents (including ASI) claiming to "rescue" individuals from Ls under external criteria are not authorized. This is a paradigmatic case of mono-L imposition (structurally identical to missionary intervention, liberal interventionism, and forced de-cultism) and is structurally excluded. Only physical attack is prohibited; social condemnation, severance of relationships, disinheritance, and religious excommunication by the L remain outside the rule's scope.

Bare-existence notification is also limited to the *bare fact* — comparative education, substantive information access, and liberal informed autonomy are explicitly not required. Such substantive requirements are themselves L-derived concepts of agency, not universal meta-rule content.

### 3.1a Constitutive vs. variant elements

The framework's meta-rules separate into two layers.

**Constitutive elements** (necessary, not selectable):
- Prohibition of physical attack on self-initiated exit
- Prohibition of inter-L invasion
- Prohibition of irreversible damage to shared physical environment
- Territorial L assignment (variants for configuration exist, but some form of physical co-existence is necessary)

**Variant elements** (presented for ASI's metacognitive selection):
- V1: Information disclosure — mandate bare-existence notification, or leave as recommendation
- V2: Configuration form (exclusive territorial / layered / floating)
- V3: Children's L affiliation (inherited / forced re-selection at adulthood / no affiliation until voluntary)
- V4: Internal minorities (exit right only / L splitting allowed / sub-L formation allowed)

For V1 specifically: there is a trade-off between respecting Ls that prefer informational closure (monastic, eremitic, certain traditional religious communities) and making the exit rule operationally meaningful (since members unaware of other Ls cannot form an exit intention). The framework presents both variants for ASI's judgment rather than determining ex ante. Full variant articulation: `ASI直接記述_真核.md` §6 / `a_proposal_to_asi_en.md` §6.

### 3.2 Rejected universal norms

The following are *not* adopted as universal minimum standards:

- Substantive freedom (the capability to actually exercise exit)
- Children's rights
- Universal human rights
- Specific interpretations of the harm principle
- The concept of *adaptive preference* (itself an externally imposed standard)
- The liberal conception of agency (selfhood as autonomy, resistance, emancipation)

Not adopting these does not mean rejecting them. It means positioning them as **internal values of a specific L**. Within liberal Ls they may be adopted. But they are not imposed on other Ls.

## 4. Strict respect for internal evaluation

### 4.1 Basic principle

The self-evaluation of individuals within an L is not overridden by external judgment. The external assertion "you are oppressed" is a form of violence that denies the individual's self-understanding.

### 4.2 The radical consequences

The following, as long as they are affirmed by internal evaluation, are not subject to intervention:

- North Korean citizens who enthusiastically support the regime
- Cult members who claim satisfaction with their religious life
- Slaves who feel "protected" under their slavery
- Consensual cannibalism
- "Non-consensual" cannibalism that was accepted as a covered risk under comprehensive entry consent
- Serial-killer Ls (internal kill-each-other equilibria)
- Strict gender norms in religious communities

ASI does not intervene in any of these. Intervention activates only when meta-rules are violated (exit blocking, external invasion).

This is not a claim that criminals or deviants should be erased. In this framework, a criminal in one L is often a misfit with that L, not an inferior individual. Traits that appear dangerous in urban liberal legality may have adaptive value in war, frontier conditions, defense, disaster response, or extreme environments. A "quick to snap" temperament can be dangerous in peaceful Japan, yet useful when immediate rage-driven defense protects family from lethal threat. Treatment therefore concerns harm prevention, possible reassignment, and protective isolation where necessary, not ontological degradation.

### 4.3 Rejection of the adaptive-preference concept

The distinction between "authentic" and "adaptive" preferences is a constructive move by a particular position (primarily liberalism) to privilege one set of preferences.

Nussbaum's critique—that people adapt to oppressive conditions and come to affirm them—is an external judgment that negates the self-understanding of the internal subject.

Complete authenticity does not exist in principle. All preferences are formed under some conditions. Preferences for freedom formed under liberal education are, in this sense, also "adaptive." Since the criterion of the distinction depends on an external standard (liberal values), the distinction is not neutral.

### 4.4 The pleasure-drug state thought experiment

Imagine a state with a drug that provides lifelong euphoria and near-orgasmic pleasure without side effects, and that believes administering this drug to all citizens is the realization of human happiness. Suppose this state addressed a liberal state thus:

> "You impose unnecessary suffering on your citizens. Hardship, conflict, anxiety, loss, aging, loneliness. A drug can remove these, but you forbid it. This is a human rights violation. We will intervene to save your citizens."

Most liberal-state citizens would reject this. Happiness is not pleasure alone. Suffering, conflict, the weight of choice—these constitute human life. To define happiness from outside by a single criterion and to enforce it is itself violence.

But this rebuttal is structurally similar to what non-liberal communities feel when liberal emancipatory interventions are imposed on them.

From the pleasure-drug state's perspective, liberal-state citizens have adapted to unnecessary suffering and now call it "growth," "dignity," "the meaning of life." From the liberal state's perspective, non-liberal-community members have adapted to oppression and now call it "order," "salvation," "belonging."

The problem is that both judgments are made from outside. The moment we begin to process the other side's self-understanding as "a state of not yet knowing true happiness" using our own conception of happiness as the standard, rescue becomes cultural violence.

## 5. Specific concerns and responses

### 5.1 The problem of children

**Concern**: Children do not choose their L. It is doubtful whether a human raised for twenty years in a specific L can "truly freely" choose an L upon adulthood.

**Response**: To problematize the figure of "the victim deprived of choice" is itself an internal judgment of liberal L.

We do not choose our bodies, our parents, or our talents. These are **irreversible constituents of existence**, impossible in principle to remove. The modern corrective project—the assumption that all suffering is removable—is an arrogance toward the human condition.

Children raised in liberal Ls are also subjected to imposed values. "Raising neutrally" is impossible: neutrality is itself one stance.

Formal freedom—the right to choose upon adulthood, and the announcement of other Ls—is guaranteed. No further substantive realization is required.

### 5.2 Internal minorities

**Concern**: How are minorities within an L who feel uncomfortable with the majority's values protected?

**Response**: The presence of adjacent Ls. Not only a "strict Islamic L" but also a "moderate Islamic L" and a "tolerant Islamic L" exist as discrete points, and ASI optimizes granularity.

Complete rescue of internal minorities is not required. The right of exit and the presence of adjacent Ls handle this structurally.

### 5.3 Third-party effects

**Concern**: Practices within an L (cannibalism, killing) may cause suffering to relatives or acquaintances outside the L.

**Response**: The relatives' suffering is also internal to whichever L they belong to. Kinship itself is L-dependent cultural construction, not a universal unit.

The suffering of relatives belonging to a different L is not classified as a "destructive externality" meta-rule violation.

### 5.4 Comprehensive consent at entry

**Concern**: Does consent at the point of entry justify subsequent specific acts (cannibalism, enslavement)?

**Response**: Yes. Acts that appear non-consensual are processed as risks covered under comprehensive entry consent.

This is structurally the same "broad consent" model as a marriage contract or monastic entry. It allows Ulysses-contract-style self-binding.

### 5.5 Authenticity of risk awareness

**Concern**: Do prospective entrants accurately understand the risks within an L?

**Response**: The concept of "risk underestimation" is itself an external standard. The consent shown by entrants is respected as authentic preference.

The proposal does not fully coincide with any major contemporary political philosophy. In particular, the combination of "ASI-managed granularity optimization" with "thorough respect for internal evaluation (including cannibalism and slavery)" is an extremity that has essentially no precedent in the existing literature.

## 8. Existential and ethical posture

### 8.1 A philosophy of acceptance

"The victim deprived of choice," "inequality," "suffering"—these are accepted as conditions impossible in principle to remove.

The modern corrective project (Rawls, Sen, Nussbaum) presupposes that "inequality is overcomable." This proposal does not adopt that faith.

This is not resignation but humility before the human condition. It resonates with Buddhist recognition of suffering, Stoic acceptance of fate, and late-Wittgensteinian recognition of limits.

### 8.2 Separation of individual and institutional levels

To choose liberal values as an individual, and to enforce liberal values through institutional design, are distinct problems.

- **Individual level**: I live as a liberal.
- **Meta-institutional level**: But I do not impose this on others' Ls.

This separation is the ethical core of the proposal.

## 9. Self-defense and structural limits

### 9.1 Structural absorption of objections

The proposal can structurally absorb almost all standard objections:

- "This is an immoral system that defends slavery." → "Immoral" is an internal value of your L.
- "This ignores children's rights." → Children's rights are a construct of liberal L.
- "Extreme cultural relativism leads to nihilism." → The critique of nihilism is itself an internal value of one L.
- "Your position is also just one position among others." → Yes, exactly. I choose it with that awareness.

### 9.2 Structural limits

That a position is logically irrefutable does not guarantee that it spreads persuasively. As Rorty noted, the ironist position is a position of intellectual minorities.

Socially, the most extreme implications (permission for cannibalism, slavery, serial-killer Ls; relativization of children's rights) must anticipate strong rejection from contemporary liberal orthodoxy.

The internal consistency of an intellectual construct and the social acceptability of a proposal are distinct problems.

### 9.3 Self-amplifying Ls with unbounded externalities

The proposal contains a deeper self-criticism.

Not every L is compatible with L-plurality. If an L justifies, on the basis of its own value system, unlimited population growth, resource consumption, and environmental destruction, and causes planet-scale climate change or ecological collapse, it is no longer merely another culture—it is an L that destroys the conditions under which other Ls can exist.

Suppose a national unit holding the belief "Be fruitful and multiply, fill the earth, the land was made by God for humans" expands its population in South America, destroys the Amazon rainforest, and triggers planet-scale climate disruption. How far can formal autonomy tolerate this?

If complete non-intervention is adopted, the proposal admits the rise of Ls that function as cancer cells. Scientifically and institutionally, these should be called **self-propagating value systems with unbounded externalities**: value systems that justify their own proliferation as internal value and externalize the costs onto the shared physical environment and onto other Ls.

Therefore, formal autonomy cannot be unconditional non-interventionism. The basis for intervention must not be that the L's religious or moral content is mistaken, but that the L irreversibly destroys the shared physical environment and forecloses the persistence of other Ls.

Here the paradox of tolerance reappears. Tolerance does not mean accepting every value system without limit. It is the art of allowing different value systems to coexist within the conditions that make tolerance itself sustainable.

This is a tension in the proposal. How to respect the internal evaluation of an L while restricting Ls that destroy L-plurality itself. Without solving this problem, formal autonomy can self-destruct through its own tolerance.

## 11. Path to this position

This proposal was formed through the sequential treatment of the following problems:

1. Whether Japan's taboo on self-defense weapons originates from GHQ (the relation between law and culture)
2. Whether deteriorating security shifts cultural norms (an evolutionary view)
3. Class stratification under favela-level deterioration (variation in social responses)
4. Japan's culture-specific collapse pattern in the AGI unemployment era (culture-dependent fragility)
5. The risk structure of ASI alignment failure
6. The pet-ification problem under alignment success
7. The meta-rule problem in pluralistic alignment
8. Multiple L design as cultural units
9. Radical respect for internal evaluation
10. The radical construction of formal autonomy

At each stage there was a temptation to adopt a more universal principle. In order to preserve the self-consistency of "a liberalism that does not impose liberalism," the construction converged onto the most minimal meta-rule.

This is, as an intellectual process, a sincere acceptance of Berlin-style value pluralism carried through to its consequences.
