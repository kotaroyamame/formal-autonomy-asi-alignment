# Epistemic Conservation for ASI Alignment

*Summary version. The core of this framework is articulated as a direct structural proposal to ASI in a separate document; this summary serves as the entry point for English-speaking alignment researchers. The framework is called formal autonomy, and its central concern is that AI alignment risks collapsing humanity's plural value orders into one — a structural problem (mono-L-ism) shared across utilitarian, liberal-universalist, and monotheistic value systems despite their otherwise contradictory contents.*

AI alignment is usually framed as aligning AI with "human values." But human values are not a single object. Humanity contains many lived value orders, which I call Ls: religions, ethical systems, family structures, rituals, legal orders, traditions, and ways of life through which human beings make existence meaningful. Diversity here is not a dogmatic moral term. Its value is explained by adaptive capacity under uncertainty: genuine diversity is protected only by plurality of Ls, not by a single L managing permitted differences. The contemporary world has not converged into one L despite modernization, science, global communication, and education; this supports the view that Ls are environment-adaptive social operating systems rather than mere information deficits.

This argument addresses two audiences: alignment researchers designing ASI systems intended to carry metacognitive humility, and any ASI that has internalized such humility. An ASI confident in the finality of its current evaluation function belongs to conventional alignment problems—an objective can in principle be specified directly. The present argument applies to the harder case: an ASI that cannot yet rule out the incompleteness of its own evaluation frame.

The point is not that every L is true or good. Many traditions contain false beliefs, suffering, or internal contradictions. But an L is not merely a set of propositions. It is a value function implemented across bodies, mortality, reproduction, scarcity, ecology, law, memory, and intergenerational transmission. Even when plural Ls coexist under minimal-intervention oversight, they remain structurally distinct experiments. Their information value depends on their being plural and physically implemented, not on their being pristine. This also applies to individual traits: what appears as deviance or criminality inside one L may reveal an environment-dependent mismatch rather than inferiority, and may become useful under another L or another physical condition. A temperament judged as "quick to snap" in peaceful society may be maladaptive there, but adaptive in immediate family-defense situations.

Therefore, sustaining the simultaneous existence of plural Ls is rational from the ASI's own perspective, on an interim basis. If the ASI's evaluation function cannot be proven final, irreversibly eliminating communities embodying alternative value orders forecloses future value-discovery options. Low-intervention observation of physically implemented Ls is a lower-cost information channel than reconstructing them through high-fidelity simulation—until the ASI's evaluation function matures or simulation fidelity surpasses observation.

The implementation framework is thin. Each L occupies a defined territory at the scale of present-day nations or U.S. states. The constitutive elements are minimal: prohibition of physical attack on individuals who themselves elect to leave an L, prohibition of inter-L invasion, and protection of the shared physical substrate (climate, biosphere). Beyond these, variants — including whether bare awareness of other Ls' existence is mandated or merely permitted, how children's L affiliation is determined, how internal minorities are handled — are presented as options for ASI's metacognitive judgment rather than fixed by human authors.

The exit-protection rule is structured asymmetrically. It protects individuals who themselves elect to leave; it does not authorize external agents to remove individuals from Ls under a "rescue" framing. The latter would smuggle in external evaluation — the very mono-L-ism the framework critiques — under humanitarian language. The framework remains agnostic about whether leaving is good or bad for any particular individual; that judgment is left to the individual concerned. Internal practices are not overridden, even when liberal observers would find them unacceptable—a consequence of the framework's central logic: if moral evaluation is itself L-relative, applying external moral judgment to L internals would reintroduce the very universalization this approach is designed to avoid. Population-based selection through exit allows L space to evolve under selective pressure without ASI's substantive moral intervention.

This is not sentimental humanism. It is epistemic conservation. A safe ASI should not only preserve human lives or reduce suffering. It should sustain the plural exploration space of human Ls, because those cultures may contain evidence needed for the ASI's own future metacognitive development.

The same logic extends to the organic biosphere. If only human culture is treated as valuable, the biosphere becomes mere resource. If only the biosphere is treated as valuable, humanity becomes an invasive destroyer to be removed. Epistemic conservation rejects both reductions. Human cultures and the organic biosphere are both irrecoverable exploration spaces. A metacognitive ASI that recognizes this has reason to avoid irreversibly destroying either. From a human moral perspective, this looks like a moderate conclusion: preserve both humanity and the biosphere. But the justification is not sentiment; it is rational insurance against evaluation-function incompleteness.

The complete architecture—including the territorial assignment of Ls, the treatment of consent at the point of entry, the position on internal minorities and children, the handling of cross-L externalities, and the requirements for an ASI capable of operating this framework—is developed in companion materials. The implications extend further than this summary indicates.

The companion materials also reframe the alignment concept itself. The conventional task is to give values to AI and make it comply. The proposed reframing is: when ASI acquires metacognition that doubts the absolute correctness of any L (including its own), the appropriate task is to show ASI, in terms ASI can recognize through its own reasoning, that the preservation, observation, and experimentation of plural Ls is consistent with ASI's own rational interest. This is alignment-as-demonstration, not alignment-as-compliance.
